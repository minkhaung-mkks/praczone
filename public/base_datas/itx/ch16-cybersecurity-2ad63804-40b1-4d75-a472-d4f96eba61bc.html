Chapter 16: Cybersecurity - Detailed Study Guide

Overview of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity involves protecting systems, networks, and data from cyberattacks. It aims to ensure the availability, integrity, and confidentiality of data, often referred to as the CIA triad. The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) defines cybersecurity as the prevention, protection, and restoration of systems to guard against unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction.

CIA Triad:

  1. Confidentiality:
    • Ensures that sensitive data is accessed only by authorized users.
    • Example: Banks protecting customer information to prevent exposure of private data.
  2. Integrity:
    • Ensures that information is accurate and not altered by unauthorized individuals.
    • Example: Your grades in a school’s system must reflect your performance without unauthorized changes.
  3. Availability:
    • Ensures that information is available to authorized users when needed.
    • Example: A Denial-of-Service (DoS) attack can reduce system availability, as happened during the Facebook outage in October 2021.

Brief History of Cybersecurity Events

  • 1981: Development of the Internet’s core technologies (TCP/IP). These protocols lacked security considerations, making future cybersecurity challenges inevitable.
  • 1982-1983: The “Gang of 414’s” (six teenagers) broke into high-profile systems using default passwords. This event helped push for the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act of 1986.
  • 1988: The Morris Worm was one of the first large-scale Internet attacks, affecting 10% of the network and leading to the establishment of CERT/CC (Computer Emergency Response Team) by Carnegie Mellon University.
  • 2000: The ILOVEYOU Virus from the Philippines infected millions of computers, revealing the global nature of cybersecurity threats.
  • 2005-2007: Retailer Attacks compromised millions of credit card details using SQL injection attacks. These attacks highlighted the importance of secure software development practices.

The Basic Information Security Model

Cybersecurity involves safeguarding assets (data, systems) from threats by mitigating vulnerabilities. The model can be broken down into:
  • Assets: Anything valuable that needs protection, such as personal data, intellectual property, etc. The challenge with assets is that they are intangible and duplicable, making theft harder to detect.
  • Threats: Methods or actions that exploit vulnerabilities. For example, malware, viruses, phishing attacks, or worms.
  • Vulnerabilities: Weaknesses in systems that threats can exploit. This could include software flaws, lack of encryption, or poor password practices. Vulnerabilities are cataloged through the Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) list.
  • Controls: Measures taken to protect assets, including physical, procedural, and technical controls. Physical controls include locks and badges; procedural controls include policies and training, while technical controls are firewalls, encryption, etc.

Cyber Hygiene

Good cyber hygiene practices reduce the risk of a successful cyberattack. These practices include:
  • Regularly updating software
  • Using strong, unique passwords
  • Avoiding public Wi-Fi for sensitive transactions
  • Installing antivirus software
  • Regularly backing up important files
  • Employing two-factor authentication
Cyber hygiene minimizes vulnerabilities, but people often remain the weakest link in cybersecurity due to poor practices, such as falling for social engineering tactics (e.g., phishing attacks).

Teams in Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity involves both offensive and defensive strategies:
  • Red Teams: These teams simulate attacks to test the defenses of an organization. They identify vulnerabilities by using the same methods as malicious actors.
  • Blue Teams: Focus on defense and ensuring that systems are secure. They monitor systems for threats and respond to cyber incidents.
Both teams work to strengthen an organization’s security posture by identifying weaknesses and reinforcing defenses.

Important Concepts and Terminology

  • Asset: Anything of value that needs protection (e.g., data).
  • Brute Force Attack: Repeatedly attempting to guess passwords or keys to gain access to a system.
  • Malware: Malicious software designed to harm or exploit systems.
  • Phishing: Pretending to be a trustworthy entity to steal sensitive data, like passwords.
  • Social Engineering: Manipulating people into providing confidential information.
  • Threat: Anything that can exploit a vulnerability and harm an asset.
  • Virus: A type of malware that spreads by attaching itself to other programs.
  • Vulnerability: A weakness in the system that can be exploited by threats.
  • Worm: Self-replicating malware that spreads without user interaction.

🏦 Introduction to Macroeconomics 🌎

Macroeconomics studies the economy as a whole, focusing on large-scale economic factors and how they interact.
Macroeconomists focus on three main aspects of the economy to understand its health and to guide policy decisions:
  • 💰 Output ( GDP )
  • 📈 Prices (Inflation )
  • 💼 Unemployment Rate

💰 Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

What is GDP?

Gross Domestic Product (GDP) is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific time period. It is a key indicator used to gauge the health of a country's economy.

🧩 Components of GDP

GDP can be broken down into four main components:
Component Description Example
Consumption Household spending on goods and services. Buying a new car
Investment Spending on capital goods that will be used for future production. Building a factory
Government Government expenditures on goods and services. Defense spending
Net Exports Exports minus imports. Selling goods abroad

📊 Nominal GDP vs. Real GDP

Nominal GDP

  • 📖 Definition: Nominal GDP is the value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders in a specific period, using current prices.
  • ❗Importance: It reflects the value of production but can be misleading due to inflation or deflation.

Real GDP

  • 📖 Definition: Real GDP adjusts for changes in price level, providing a more accurate measure of an economy’s output by using constant prices.
  • ❗Importance: It allows comparisons across time by removing the effect of price changes.
Nominal GDP Real GDP
Measured in current prices Measured in constant prices (base year)
Affected by inflation/deflation Not affected by changes in price level
Used to compare current output Used to compare output over time

📈 GDP Deflator

  • 📖 Definition: The GDP Deflator is a measure of the price level of all goods and services included in GDP. It reflects the change in the average price of goods and services.
  • ≒ Formula:
    GDP Deflator = Nominal GDP Real GDP × 100 GDP Deflator = Nominal GDP Real GDP × 100 "GDP Deflator"=("Nominal GDP")/("Real GDP")xx100\text{GDP Deflator} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100GDP Deflator=Nominal GDPReal GDP×100

🌍 GDP Per Capita

  • 📖 Definition: GDP per capita is the GDP divided by the population, giving an average economic output per person.
  • ❗Importance: It is a useful measure for comparing the standard of living between different countries.

⚠️ Limitations of GDP

  • ❗❗Doesn't measure distribution: GDP doesn’t show income inequality within a country.
  • ❗❗Ignores non-market transactions: Unpaid work, such as household labor, isn’t included.
  • ❗❗Doesn’t account for environmental costs: GDP growth may come at the cost of environmental degradation.

💼 Income Approach

  • 📖 Description: Calculates GDP by summing all incomes earned in the production of goods and services.
  • Includes: Wages, rents, interest, and profits.

🏭 Production (Value-Added) Approach

  • Description: Measures GDP by summing the value added at each stage of production.
  • Value Added: The difference between the value of output and the value of intermediate goods used in production.

📈 Price/Inflation

What is Inflation?

  • 📖 Definition: Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, and subsequently, purchasing power falls. Central banks attempt to limit inflation—and avoid deflation—to keep the economy running smoothly.
  • Why It Matters:
    • A moderate level of inflation is normal in a growing economy.
    • High inflation erodes purchasing power, meaning each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services.
    • Deflation, the opposite of inflation, can lead to decreased consumer spending, as people delay purchases in anticipation of lower prices.

Types of Inflation

  • Demand-Pull Inflation: Occurs when the demand for goods and services exceeds their supply, leading to higher prices.
  • Cost-Push Inflation: Results from an increase in the cost of production, such as wages or raw materials, which leads to higher prices for finished goods.
  • Built-In Inflation: Linked to adaptive expectations; as prices rise, workers demand higher wages, which in turn causes prices to rise further.

🌡️ Measuring Inflation: Consumer Price Index (CPI)

What is CPI?

  • 📖 Definition: The Consumer Price Index (CPI) is a measure that examines the weighted average of prices of a basket of consumer goods and services, such as transportation, food, and medical care.
  • Purpose: CPI is used to calculate inflation by comparing the price changes in this basket of goods and services over time.

How CPI is Calculated

  • Base Year: CPI calculation begins with a base year, where the index is set to 100.
  • Basket of Goods: The government selects a representative basket of goods and services that reflects the consumption habits of the average household.
  • Price Collection: Prices of items in the basket are collected regularly (monthly, quarterly) to calculate the index.

💸 Cost of Living (COL)

What is Cost of Living?

  • 📖 Definition: Cost of Living (COL) refers to the amount of money needed to cover basic expenses such as housing, food, taxes, and healthcare in a particular place and time.
  • Purpose: COL is used to compare how expensive it is to live in different regions or over time. It's particularly useful for understanding regional differences in expenses and for adjusting salaries.

COL Example

City Housing Groceries Transportation Healthcare Total COL
City A $1,000 $300 $150 $200 $1,650
City B $1,500 $350 $200 $250 $2,300
  • Comparison:
    • City B has a higher COL compared to City A, meaning living in City B is more expensive.

Inflation vs. Cost of Living

  • Inflation: Refers to the rate at which prices for goods and services rise across the entire economy.
  • Cost of Living: Refers to the amount of money needed to sustain a certain standard of living in a specific place.

Causes and Effects of Inflation

Causes:

  • Demand-Pull Inflation: Increased demand for goods and services.
  • Cost-Push Inflation: Rising costs of production.
  • Monetary Policy: Excessive money supply can lead to inflation.
  • Expectations: If people expect inflation, they may act in ways that cause it.

Effects:

  • Reduced Purchasing Power: Money buys less, leading to decreased consumer spending.
  • Income Redistribution: Inflation can hurt savers and benefit borrowers.
  • Menu Costs: Businesses face costs from changing prices frequently.

💼 Unemployment

What is Unemployment?

  • 📖 Definition: Unemployment occurs when people who are able and willing to work are unable to find employment. It’s a key indicator of economic health, as high unemployment often signals economic distress.
  • ❗ Why It Matters: Unemployment impacts income, consumer spending, and overall economic growth. It’s a central concern in macroeconomics because it reflects the utilization of labor resources in the economy.

📊 Types of Unemployment

Type Meaning Example
Frictional Short-term unemployment occurring when people are between jobs or entering the labor force for the first time. A recent college graduate searching for a job.
Also known as Search and Wait Unemployment
Structural Unemployment resulting from industrial reorganization, typically due to technological changes, making some skills obsolete. Factory workers losing jobs due to automation.
Also known as Mismatch Unemployment
Cyclical Unemployment linked to the economic cycle; it rises during recessions and falls during expansions. Layoffs during a recession.
Also known as Demand-Deficient Unemployment
Seasonal Unemployment Unemployment related to seasonal work, such as agricultural or holiday jobs. Retail workers hired for the holiday season being laid off afterward.
Also Known as Seasonal Layoff

🔍 Measuring Unemployment

  • Labor Force: The labor force includes all people aged 16 and over who are either employed or actively seeking employment.
  • Not in Labor Force: Those not actively seeking work, such as students, retirees, and discouraged workers, are not considered part of the labor force.

🛠️ Unemployment Rate

  • 📖 Definition: The unemployment rate is the percentage of the labor force that is unemployed and actively seeking employment.

🎯 Natural Rate of Unemployment

  • 📖 Definition: The natural rate of unemployment is the level of unemployment that persists in an economy when it is at full employment, considering frictional and structural unemployment.
  • Full Employment: Does not mean zero unemployment but rather that the only unemployment is frictional or structural, with no cyclical unemployment.

📉 Implications of Unemployment

  • Economic:
    • Reduced Income: Unemployment leads to lower household income and reduced consumer spending.
    • Lower GDP: High unemployment generally correlates with lower GDP, as fewer people are working and contributing to the economy.
    • Government Spending: Unemployment often leads to increased government spending on social programs like unemployment benefits.
  • Social:
    • Increased Poverty: Long-term unemployment can lead to poverty and reduced living standards.
    • Social Instability: High unemployment can lead to social unrest and decreased quality of life.
  • Psychological:
    • Mental Health: Long-term unemployment can lead to stress, anxiety, and depression.
    • Self-Worth: Unemployment can negatively impact a person’s sense of purpose and self-esteem.

🔍 Labor Force and Labor Force Participation Rate

👨‍🦱 Labor Force

  • 📖 Definition: The labor force includes all individuals aged 16 and over who are either employed or actively seeking employment. It represents the total supply of labor available for producing goods and services in an economy.
  • Who is Included?
    • 💼 Employed: Those currently working for pay.
    • 😵‍💫 Unemployed: Those not working but actively seeking a job.
  • Who is Not Included?
    • 😔 Discouraged Workers: Individuals who have stopped looking for work because they believe no jobs are available.
    • 🧑‍🎓 Students, Retirees, Homemakers: Individuals not actively seeking employment due to schooling, retirement, or household responsibilities.
    • 💠 Institutionalized Persons and Military Personnel: Individuals in prisons, mental health facilities, or active military duty.

😀 Eligible Population

  • 📖 Definition: The eligible population includes all individuals within a country who are of working age (typically 16 years and older), excluding those institutionalized or in the military.

Labor Force Participation Rate

  • 📖 Definition: The labor force participation rate is the percentage of the eligible population that is either employed or actively seeking employment.
  • ≒ Formula:
    Labor Force Participation Rate = Labor Force Eligible Population × 100 Labor Force Participation Rate = Labor Force Eligible Population × 100 "Labor Force Participation Rate"=("Labor Force")/("Eligible Population")xx100\text{Labor Force Participation Rate} = \frac{\text{Labor Force}}{\text{Eligible Population}} \times 100Labor Force Participation Rate=Labor ForceEligible Population×100
  • 📑 Example: If the eligible population is 800 and the labor force is 600, the participation rate is 75%.

📉 Business Cycle

What is the Business Cycle?

  • 📖Definition: The business cycle refers to the natural rise and fall of economic growth that occurs over time. It consists of periods of expansion (growth) and contraction (decline) in an economy.

Phases of the Business Cycle

  1. 📈Expansion:
    • 📖Characteristics: Increasing economic activity, rising GDP, low unemployment, and growing consumer confidence and spending.
    • 📑Example: A period where businesses are investing in new projects, hiring more workers, and consumers are spending more due to rising incomes.
  2. ⤴️Peak:
    • 📖Characteristics: The highest point of economic activity before a downturn. Unemployment is at its lowest, and inflation may start to rise due to higher demand.
    • 📑Example: When the economy is booming, and output is at its maximum sustainable level.
  3. 📉Contraction (Recession):
    • 📖Characteristics: Decreasing economic activity, falling GDP, increasing unemployment, and reduced consumer spending and investment.
    • 📑Example: A period marked by layoffs, reduced production, and lower consumer spending, often triggered by a decline in demand.
  4. ⤵️Trough:
    • 📖Characteristics: The lowest point of the economic cycle, where economic activity is at its weakest. Unemployment is high, and inflation is low or negative.
    • 📑Example: The economy hits bottom, and there is minimal growth, but it also sets the stage for the next phase of recovery.
  5. ↻Recovery:
    • 📖Characteristics: The economy begins to grow again, with increasing GDP, falling unemployment, and rising consumer and business confidence.
    • 📑Example: Businesses start to hire again, production ramps up, and consumer spending picks up, leading to economic expansion.

❗Why is the Business Cycle Important? ❗

  • Impact on Policy: Understanding the business cycle helps policymakers implement appropriate fiscal and monetary policies to manage economic growth and curb inflation or unemployment.
  • Investment Decisions: Investors use knowledge of the business cycle to make informed decisions about buying or selling assets, aiming to maximize returns during expansions and minimize losses during contractions.

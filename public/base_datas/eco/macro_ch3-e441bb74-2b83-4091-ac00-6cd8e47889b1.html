📊 AD-AS Model Overview

What is the AD-AS Model?

  • 📖 Definition: The AD-AS (Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply) model is a key framework to understand how an economy functions. It represents the total demand (AD) and total supply (AS) of goods and services in a country.
  • Purpose: This model helps explain fluctuations in output, price levels, and employment, providing insight into how government policies and external factors impact the economy.

🔄 Shifts in Aggregate Demand and Supply

Aggregate Demand (AD)

  • 📈 Description: Aggregate demand represents the total quantity of goods and services that households, businesses, and the government want to purchase at different price levels.
  • 🧩 Key Concept: Negative relationship between price level and quantity demanded—when prices rise, people tend to buy less.
Factor Effect on AD
Consumer Confidence Higher confidence → AD shifts right (increase)
Taxes Higher taxes → AD shifts left (decrease)
Government Spending Higher spending → AD shifts right (increase)

Aggregate Supply (AS)

  • 📉 Description: Aggregate supply shows the total quantity of goods and services firms are willing to produce at different price levels.
  • 🧩 Key Concept: Positive relationship between price level and output—when prices rise, firms are more willing to produce.
Factor Effect on AS
Resource Costs Higher costs → AS shifts left (decrease)
Technological Advances New tech → AS shifts right (increase)

⚖️ Equilibrium and Market Adjustments

  • Equilibrium: The point where AD intersects AS, representing the economy’s output (Y) and price level (P) when supply matches demand.
    🧠 Example:
    • Surplus: When AS > AD, firms reduce prices to sell excess output.
    • Shortage: When AD > AS, firms increase prices due to high demand.

🌍 Case Studies: Economic Events

Case 1: Russia-Ukraine War Impact on Thailand

  • Supply Shock: The war increased oil prices, causing firms to pay more for resources.
    • Result: AS shifted left, leading to higher prices (inflation) and lower output (recession).
Effect Description
Price Level Increase (Inflation)
Output (GDP) Decrease (Recession)
Unemployment Increase

Case 2: China's Economic Opening

  • Demand Increase: China lifted lockdowns, leading to increased purchases of Thai products.
    • Result: AD shifted right, resulting in higher output and prices.
Effect Description
Price Level Increase (Inflation)
Output (GDP) Increase
Unemployment Decrease

🏛️ Government Policies

Fiscal Policies

  • 📖 Definition: Fiscal policies are tools used by the government to influence the economy through changes in taxes and spending.
Policy Description Effect on AD
Expansionary Fiscal Policy Lower taxes, higher government spending Increases AD (Boost economy)
Contractionary Fiscal Policy Higher taxes, reduced spending Decreases AD (Reduce inflation)

Monetary Policies

  • 📖 Definition: Monetary policies are managed by the central bank to control the money supply and interest rates.
Policy Description Effect on AD
Expansionary Monetary Policy Lower interest rates, buy government bonds Increases AD (Boost economy)
Contractionary Monetary Policy Raise interest rates, sell government bonds Decreases AD (Control inflation)

🔄 Choosing the Right Policy

To combat inflation or recession, governments must decide between:
  1. Expansionary Policies: To fight recessions by increasing AD, boosting production, and reducing unemployment, but with the risk of higher inflation.
  2. Contractionary Policies: To control inflation by reducing AD, though it may increase unemployment and lower output.

📈 Demand-Pull vs. Cost-Push Inflation

Demand-Pull Inflation

  • 📖 Definition: Occurs when aggregate demand in an economy exceeds aggregate supply, driving prices up.
  • Example: Increased consumer confidence causes more spending, shifting AD to the right.

Cost-Push Inflation

  • 📖 Definition: Happens when rising production costs (e.g., resource prices) lead firms to increase prices, even when demand hasn't changed.
  • Example: A rise in oil prices due to war, which shifts the AS curve left, resulting in higher prices and lower output.

🎯 Practical Policy Applications

  1. War Between Russia and Ukraine: The Thai economy faces a dual problem of inflation and unemployment. The government has two choices:
    • Expansionary Fiscal or Monetary Policy:
      • Purpose: Focus on reducing unemployment by boosting output (Y) and demand.
      • Trade-off: This will further increase inflation.
    • Contractionary Fiscal or Monetary Policy:
      • Purpose: Focus on controlling inflation by reducing AD.
      • Trade-off: This will likely worsen unemployment.
  2. China's Opening Up: With China opening, Thailand benefits from increased demand for its goods and services, but this could lead to inflation. Here, the government may choose:
    • Contractionary Policies to control inflation and prevent the economy from overheating.

🚫 Limitations of Government Policies

  • Fiscal and Monetary Policies Only Affect AD: While government policies can control aggregate demand (AD), they do not directly affect aggregate supply (AS). If the problem is cost-push inflation (e.g., resource prices increase), the government has limited tools.
    • Example: In the case of the Russia-Ukraine war causing oil prices to rise, the government can shift AD to reduce inflation, but it cannot directly reduce the costs of resources like oil.
